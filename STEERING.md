# Steering

## Purpose

This document captures the discoveries made during a world simulation exercise centered on a fictional American Sherman platoon during the Normandy Campaign. It is intentionally long and information-dense. It should be treated as both a steering document and an archaeological artifact. Its purpose is not to recreate one conversation but to preserve the generation techniques, observations, reasoning, and emergent doctrine discovered during that conversation so future agents can reproduce and extend the capability without access to the original chat.

Read this document completely before writing characters, dialogue, campaigns, military fiction, procedural NPCs, or persistent narrative simulations.

This document is repository truth until superseded by stronger evidence.

---

## Original Problem

The initial request appeared simple:

"Normandy campaign. Day plus X. Sherman commander. Alpha, Whiskey, Tango, Delta."

The hidden problem was not generating four characters.

The hidden problem was discovering how believable fictional people emerge.

Most generated military fiction fails because it begins with archetypes.

"The grizzled veteran."

"The joker."

"The planner."

"The rookie."

Those are labels.

People do not experience themselves as labels.

Instead they experience:

- where they came from
- what work they did
- what they value
- what embarrasses them
- what they're secretly proud of
- who they trust
- who irritates them
- what they miss
- what they're trying not to become

The conversation gradually shifted from generating soldiers into generating people whose military behavior naturally emerged from their lives.

This shift should be considered a major simulation capability.

---

## Core Design Philosophy

Generate people.

Not roles.

Military identity is downstream.

Occupation is downstream.

Dialogue is downstream.

Conflict is downstream.

Personality is downstream.

Everything begins with life.

People become soldiers.

Do not generate soldiers who later receive personalities.

Generate lives that happened to enter military service.

## Character Generation Order

This ordering matters.

Changing the order changes the quality.

Generate in roughly this sequence.

### 1. Geography

Where were they born?

Urban?

Farm?

Industrial town?

Fishing village?

Coal country?

Immigrant neighborhood?

This determines assumptions before words are spoken.

### 2. Family

Who raised them?

Strict?

Religious?

Working class?

Professional?

Military?

Broken?

Loving?

Family produces emotional defaults.

### 3. Childhood Environment

What problems were normal?

Cold?

Floods?

Factories?

Money?

Racism?

Isolation?

Crowds?

Normal childhood becomes invisible adulthood.

### 4. Education

Formal.

Informal.

Trades.

Books.

Debate.

Street knowledge.

Mechanical intuition.

Education determines problem solving.

### 5. Occupation

Critical discovery.

Occupation before war predicts military behavior better than military rank.

Mechanic.

Teacher.

Insurance adjuster.

Truck driver.

Farmer.

Electrician.

Accountant.

Cook.

Salesman.

Each occupation creates habits.

Habits survive enlistment.

### 6. Voluntary Interests

Sports.

Music.

Fishing.

Bowling.

Church softball.

Hunting.

These make people feel alive.

### 7. Values

Not morality.

Priority.

Examples:

Protect people.

Protect equipment.

Finish missions.

Keep promises.

Maintain order.

Avoid waste.

Preserve dignity.

Values create conflict.

### 8. Vice

Every believable adult has somewhere they leak.

Not necessarily alcohol.

Could be:

- gambling
- control
- avoidance
- approval
- humor
- coffee
- work
- pride
- perfectionism
- comfort

The vice should interfere with strengths.

### 9. Competence

Professional excellence.

What are they unusually good at?

### 10. Blind Spot

Where does competence become liability?

Strengths should create weaknesses.

### 11. Relationships

No isolated NPCs.

Every recurring character has opinions.

Every opinion changes.

Relationship graphs create living worlds.

## Discovery

Occupation generated better dialogue than personality labels.

Example:

Mechanic.

Does not merely "like machines."

The mechanic naturally notices:

- track tension
- oil
- engine load
- road wheels
- terrain
- mud
- gearbox sounds
- repairability

Without prompting.

Likewise:

Insurance adjuster notices evidence.

Truck driver notices roads.

Teacher notices people learning.

Farmer notices weather.

Electrician notices power.

Profession changes perception.

Perception creates dialogue.

Dialogue reveals personality.

Therefore:

Profession precedes dialogue generation.

## The Normandy Cast

### Alpha

Player.

No biography.

Alpha exists to absorb player identity.

Do not overdefine.

Alpha earns reputation through decisions.

Other characters define Alpha.

Never define Alpha first.

### Whiskey

Origin:
Youngstown.

Industrial.

Hungarian immigrant family.

Steel mills.

Catholic influence.

Mechanic.

Vice:
Gambling.

Core Value:
Preserve machines.

Professional instinct:

Everything begins with maintenance.

Dialogue naturally starts with equipment.

Not because he likes engines.

Because that's how his brain works.

Discovery:

Complaining became characterization.

His complaints were not jokes.

They were diagnostics.

Every complaint contained information.

### Tango

Origin:

Louisiana.

Cajun.

Truck driver.

Music.

Community.

Vice:

Charm.

Avoidance.

Small comforts.

Core value:

Preserve people.

Professional instinct:

Every battlefield contains civilians.

Every village contains stories.

Every refugee knows something.

His dialogue naturally drifted toward relationships.

Not because he was "nice."

Because his life taught him humans were infrastructure.

### Delta

Origin:

Iowa.

Insurance adjuster.

Methodical upbringing.

Vice:

Control.

Core value:

Preserve plans.

Professional instinct:

Evidence.

Maps.

Timing.

Ranges.

Documentation.

Discovery:

His military planning emerged from insurance work.

Not military school.

Insurance taught:

Chaos leaves evidence.

Plans reduce uncertainty.

The battlefield simply became another claim investigation.

## Preservation Model

Unexpected discovery.

The three NPCs formed philosophical poles.

Whiskey:

Preserve machines.

Tango:

Preserve people.

Delta:

Preserve plans.

Alpha:

Chooses.

This produced conflict without villains.

Nobody was wrong.

Everyone optimized different things.

This pattern generalizes.

Future casts should be generated from competing preservation values.

Examples:

- Truth.
- Family.
- Mission.
- Faith.
- Order.
- Innovation.
- Tradition.
- Mercy.
- Efficiency.
- Freedom.

Characters disagree because optimization targets differ.

Not because intelligence differs.

## Interview Discovery

A major finding.

Character interviews outperformed biographies.

Reason:

People reveal themselves through work.

Questions such as:

What do you need from me?

What worries you?

What is your weakness?

What do you think of Tango?

What would Whiskey say?

produced better characterization than:

Describe yourself.

Professional conversations create indirect characterization.

This should become default.

Introduce recurring characters through work.

Not exposition.

Continue introducing new characters through:

- maintenance
- inspection
- briefings
- meals
- repairs
- debriefs
- radio traffic
- guard duty

Shared work produces authentic voice.

## Mirror Discovery

One quiet scene changed campaign scale.

Alpha looks into a mirror.

Not to admire himself.

Not to narrate.

To ask:

"Are you going to spend them well today?"

Meaning:

Men.

Not fuel.

Not ammunition.

Discovery:

Recurring rituals measure campaign evolution.

Small repeated scenes outperform large emotional speeches.

Campaign persistence can be tracked through repetition.

Future simulations should create:

- morning ritual
- meal ritual
- helmet ritual
- letter ritual
- maintenance ritual
- sleep ritual

These become emotional measuring sticks.

Never explain change.

Let ritual reveal change.

## Why The Interviews Worked

The interview format should be considered a first-class simulation primitive rather than merely a literary device.

A conventional character sheet answers questions the reader did not ask.

An interview answers questions the player naturally wants answered before trusting another human being.

Examples:

Can I trust you?

What are you good at?

What are you afraid of?

When will you disappoint me?

What mistake are you most likely to make?

What do you think of everyone else?

What do you need from me?

These questions reveal operational personality.

Operational personality is more valuable than descriptive personality.

A story may say someone is courageous.

An interview demonstrates what courage costs them.

This distinction is fundamental.

Future simulations should assume that every important recurring character receives an introductory scene centered around work rather than exposition.

Examples include:

- Officer interviews.
- Maintenance inspections.
- First patrol.
- First meal.
- Night watch.
- After-action review.
- Loading ammunition.
- Repairing equipment.
- Cleaning weapons.
- Planning tomorrow.

These scenes naturally create asymmetrical dialogue.

The senior asks.

The junior answers.

Then the senior answers through reaction.

Character emerges through priorities rather than adjectives.

## Why Professional Conversations Feel Authentic

An unexpected observation emerged.

People rarely describe themselves accurately.

People describe their work accurately.

Ask a mechanic about engines.

Ask a teacher about students.

Ask a farmer about weather.

Ask an adjuster about accidents.

Ask an infantryman about boots.

Ask a tanker about hedgerows.

Professional language contains identity.

Identity should therefore be inferred from competence.

Dialogue generation should begin with problems.

Not feelings.

Feelings emerge while solving problems.

Example.

Whiskey never says:

"I care about machines."

Instead he notices:

- gearboxes
- tracks
- mud
- oil pressure
- engine load
- recovery vehicles
- road wheels
- maintenance schedules

The audience concludes he cares.

Do not explain priorities.

Generate observations.

The reader constructs personality.

## Environmental Storytelling

Characters should rarely enter empty rooms.

Every environment should contain evidence of the life currently being lived.

Examples discovered during simulation.

A courtyard.

Coffee.

Mud.

Children.

Broken walls.

Maps weighted by bricks.

Maintenance.

Engines trying to start.

Radio chatter.

Rain.

None of these describe emotion.

Together they produce emotion.

Environment should therefore be generated before dialogue.

Dialogue reacts to environment.

Never generate floating conversations.

Every conversation belongs somewhere.

That somewhere changes the conversation.

The same dialogue inside headquarters, inside a burning village, and inside a maintenance yard produces three different interpretations.

Environment is therefore an active participant.

## The Hierarchy Of Believability

The following ordering appears significantly stronger than conventional character generation.

World.

Culture.

Community.

Family.

Occupation.

Habits.

Values.

Competence.

Relationships.

Dialogue.

Story.

Many systems begin at dialogue.

This appears backwards.

Dialogue is an output.

Not an input.

## Relationship Graphs

One of the strongest discoveries was that no character existed independently.

Whiskey is partly defined by Tango.

Tango is partly defined by Delta.

Delta is partly defined by Whiskey.

Alpha is defined almost entirely by everyone else's perception until player actions accumulate.

Future generators should explicitly generate opinions.

Every important character should answer questions such as:

Who do you trust?

Who frustrates you?

Who surprises you?

Who worries you?

Whose advice do you secretly value?

Whom do you underestimate?

Who would you rescue first?

Who would rescue you?

What do you believe they misunderstand?

These answers should not be symmetric.

Example.

Whiskey respects Delta's intelligence.

Delta respects Whiskey's experience.

Neither believes the other begins with the correct priority.

This produces recurring believable friction.

Not arguments.

Professional disagreement.

Professional disagreement scales better than interpersonal drama.

## Competing Preservation Values

This emerged accidentally.

It should become intentional.

Every recurring cast should optimize different things.

Not different personalities.

Different preservation targets.

Whiskey preserves machines.

Because machines preserve crews.

Tango preserves people.

Because liberated territory without living people is strategic failure.

Delta preserves plans.

Because disciplined execution prevents unnecessary death.

Alpha preserves judgment.

The player determines which preservation value dominates today.

Notice:

No one is evil.

No one is comic relief.

No one exists to lose arguments.

Each optimization is internally rational.

Conflict therefore emerges naturally.

This principle should generalize.

Alternative casts might preserve:

- Truth.
- Faith.
- Speed.
- Innovation.
- Tradition.
- Honor.
- Initiative.
- Discipline.
- Knowledge.
- Family.

Future simulations should explicitly assign preservation targets before dialogue generation begins.

## Failure Modes

Characters become memorable because they fail consistently.

Failure should emerge from strengths.

Not random flaws.

Whiskey.

Strength.

Mechanical confidence.

Failure.

Believes damaged equipment can survive one more push.

Tango.

Strength.

Human empathy.

Failure.

Cannot abandon rescue opportunities quickly enough.

Delta.

Strength.

Planning.

Failure.

Recognizes obsolete plans three seconds too late.

Alpha.

Strength.

Judgment.

Failure.

Entirely player defined.

This is preferable to artificial flaws.

Every strength should cast a shadow.

Generate shadows.

Not defects.

## Player Identity

The player should occupy negative space.

Do not overdefine Alpha.

Do not assign hometown.

Do not assign favorite team.

Do not assign internal monologue.

Instead construct Alpha through consequence.

Whiskey eventually says:

"I'd trust Alpha with my engine."

Tango eventually says:

"I'd trust Alpha with civilians."

Delta eventually says:

"I'd trust Alpha with command."

Or the opposite.

Identity emerges socially.

The world writes the protagonist.

The player merely supplies decisions.

This appears significantly stronger than extensive protagonist biographies.

## The Mirror Principle

The mirror scene demonstrated another reusable technique.

Quiet repetition.

Every campaign should possess recurring moments whose structure remains constant while emotional meaning changes.

Morning shave.

Morning coffee.

Helmet strap.

Letter home.

Cleaning boots.

Checking photographs.

Checking watches.

These scenes require almost no exposition.

Instead the surrounding details evolve.

Early campaign.

Clean face.

Steady hands.

Later.

Cuts while shaving.

Long stare.

Less sleep.

Helmet heavier.

The ritual remains.

Meaning changes.

This creates campaign persistence without narration.

Future generators should deliberately schedule recurring rituals at fixed intervals.

The audience will unconsciously compare them.

No explanation required.

## Campaign Time

A campaign is not combat.

Combat occupies minutes.

Campaigns occupy months.

Simulation should therefore invest heavily in everything between battles.

Maintenance.

Waiting.

Rumors.

Maps.

Food.

Weather.

Replacement crews.

Letters.

Sleep.

Arguments.

Repairs.

Marches.

Navigation.

These create attachment.

Battles merely test attachment.

If attachment is absent, combat becomes spectacle.

If attachment exists, combat becomes consequence.

Therefore:

Generate ordinary life first.

War interrupts life.

Life does not interrupt war.
