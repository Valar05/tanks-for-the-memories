# Battlefield Awareness Research Strategy

The camera doctrine changes the research target.

The corpus is no longer only a record of battles, units, manuals, and vehicles. It must also capture the chain of human perception that decides what the player is allowed to know.

## Research Shift

Prioritize sources that answer:

- Who saw it?
- From where?
- Under what visibility conditions?
- How sure were they?
- How long until someone else knew?
- How did the report change as it moved upward?
- What did fear, smoke, terrain, or fatigue distort?

## High-Value Source Types

- Scout reports and reconnaissance summaries
- After-action reports that compare initial sightings to later corrections
- Memoirs with first-contact descriptions
- Radio logs and message transcripts
- Infantry and tank commander testimony about what they could actually see
- Friendly-fire and misidentification accounts
- Ambush narratives that show the first observer becoming the camera
- Training manuals on observation, reporting, and spotting discipline

## Extraction Priorities

Every candidate source should be mined for:

- observer role
- observer location
- line-of-sight constraints
- confidence level
- delay before report
- whether this was first contact or a later confirmation
- whether the report was corrected
- whether the report changed the tactical decision
- whether the report created a new memory marker

## Mechanical Questions

For each source, ask:

- What reveal should this cause?
- What uncertainty should it preserve?
- What confidence score should it assign?
- What map marker should it create?
- What delay should exist before higher HQ or the player learns the same fact?
- What mistaken identification should remain plausible until corrected?

## Corpus Expansion Rule

Prefer sources that include sensory language:

- saw
- heard
- thought
- guessed
- suspected
- couldn't make out
- through the hedge
- in the smoke
- after the shell burst

These phrases are the raw material for awareness mechanics.

## Next Acquisitions

- British, Canadian, German, and Polish observer-handbook material
- Direct memoir passages on first contact, ambushes, and bad identifications
- AARs that contain corrected contact reports
- Signal and radio logs that show stale or damaged battlefield pictures
- Interior and hatch-view references that help position the observer

## Strategic Outcome

The best sources will not only tell us what happened.
They will tell us what could be seen, what could be known, and what could only be remembered later.
