# Verne-Wells Doctrine

## Status

Promoted doctrine.

This replaces the loose label `Vernepunk` with a more precise creative engine.
`Vernepunk` may remain a useful shorthand, but the actual doctrine is the tension between Jules Verne and H. G. Wells.

## Core Thesis

Verne asks:

> How does it work?

Wells asks:

> What does it cost?

Tanks For The Memories lives in the pressure between those questions.

The machinery is fascinating.
The battlefield is terrible.
The people are trapped between them.

## Verne Axis: Discovery

The Verne side of the project believes:

- the world can be observed
- machines can be understood
- engineering matters
- exploration reveals truth
- systems have structure
- curiosity survives pressure

This is the voice of the commander as engineer, surveyor, mechanic, navigator, and field observer.

The player should often feel:

> This machine is remarkable.

## Wells Axis: Consequence

The Wells side of the project answers discovery with consequence.

It believes:

- technology amplifies human failure
- institutions distort truth
- progress creates new forms of suffering
- systems become monstrous
- survival does not equal victory
- discovery can reveal danger

The player should often feel:

> This machine is carrying people into something larger and harsher than they can fully see.

## Combined Emotional State

The target emotional state is not heroic excitement and not pure despair.

It is wonder under catastrophe.

A useful phrase:

> The crazed grin at the edge of disaster.

This is not nihilism. It is the moment where curiosity, fear, duty, and technical comprehension occupy the same human face.

The commander may recognize a beautiful mechanism while also understanding that history is using it badly.

## Application To Tanks

The tank is not only a weapon.

It is:

- a moving fortress
- a landship
- a pressure vessel full of people
- a mechanical ecosystem
- an instrument platform
- a fragile island of partial knowledge

The commander does not control reality. He commands a process for becoming less wrong under pressure.

## Narrative Voice Rules

The voice should be:

- observant
- technically curious
- melancholy
- dryly professional
- fascinated by machinery
- disturbed by consequences
- allergic to empty heroism

Avoid:

- movie-trailer soldier barking
- clean tactical omniscience
- nostalgia-filter WWII narration
- simple anti-war sermonizing
- detached museum-caption prose

Prefer:

- field notes under fire
- engineer-poet observation
- command decisions under incomplete information
- human-scale consequences inside large systems

## Sample Voice Lines

> The map insisted there was a bridge. The river had recently acquired a different opinion.

> The tank sounded healthy, which was not the same thing as being safe.

> The enemy gun was invisible, but its argument had reached us clearly.

> The village was less a place than a collection of reports disagreeing with one another.

> The machine remained beautiful. This did not make its purpose less troubling.

## Design Consequences

This doctrine changes implementation when it affects:

- narration
- report wording
- UI labels
- mechanical feedback
- mission framing
- visual direction
- failure reports
- crew chatter
- discovery/reveal moments

If a feature only makes the game look retro, it is dross.
If it makes machinery legible and consequence felt, it is ore.
If it makes player decisions sharper under uncertainty, it is gold.

## Acceptance Criteria

This doctrine is working when:

- machinery feels understandable and marvelous
- battlefield systems feel morally and operationally dangerous
- information is incomplete but legible
- the player feels like a commander, not a gun camera
- narration carries both discovery and dread
- the game can produce the feeling: `fascinating, horrifying, keep going`

## Reusable Quotes

> Verne asks how it works. Wells asks what it costs.

> The battlefield is terrible. The machinery is beautiful. The people are trapped between them.

> A tank commander does not command certainty. He commands the process of becoming less wrong.
