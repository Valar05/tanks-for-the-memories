# Crew Memory Report

This report collects replacement crews, grief, survivor memory, hesitation, survivor guilt, and command burden.

## Replacement Crew
- Bomb: the tank’s commander was wounded and replaced, making the vehicle a memory vessel rather than anonymous hardware.
- 761st Tank Battalion: long service and replenishment required adaptation under racial and institutional pressure.

## Loss of Comrades
- D-Day: The Battle for Normandy emphasizes lived loss and exhaustion.
- Heinrich Severloh preserves the other side of Omaha Beach memory.

## Leadership Burden
- Omaha Beachhead: local leaders had to command fragments when radios, men, and vehicles were missing.
- Cross-Channel Attack, Chapter IX: commanders had to act on reports that lagged reality.

## Remembered Casualties
- Operation Totalize: friendly fire became part of the remembered cost of coalition attack.
- Combat stress reaction: fatigue and stress are operational variables, not private feelings only.

## Hesitation and Survivor Guilt
- Carentan: men did not receive the charge order uniformly, producing hesitation and confusion.
- Mail and home-contact sources show how memory keeps the absent present.

## Simulation Implications
- replacement_familiarity should start low and rise slowly.
- crew_confidence should fall after casualties and recover only with repeated shared success.
- after-action reports should preserve named losses, mail delays, and the emotional cost of command.
